Big Idea: Waste Management & Recycling in a Circular Economy

Team: Mechanical & Mechatronics Engineering Undergraduates

Context: Sri Lanka

1. Why We Selected This as a "Big Idea"

The management of solid waste is one of the most critical environmental and social challenges facing Sri Lanka today. Rapid urbanization, population growth, and a rise in consumerism have led to an exponential increase in waste generation, overwhelming existing infrastructure. This topic was chosen for several key reasons:

  • National Relevance: Improper waste disposal leads to polluted land, waterways, and coastlines, directly impacting public health, tourism, and biodiversity in Sri Lanka. Landfills like Kerawalapitiya and Aruwakkalu are temporary fixes to a permanent problem.
  • Engineering Challenge: This area is rich with opportunities for mechanical and mechatronics innovation. It moves beyond simple civil engineering to complex systems involving automation, robotics, data science, and machine design.
  • Career Experience: It provides direct, hands-on experience in high-demand fields such as Industrial Automation, Robotics (for sorting), IoT (for smart collection), and Sustainable System Design. These skills are transferable to manufacturing, logistics, and beyond.
  • Economic Potential: Adopting a circular economy model—where waste is not just disposed of but is a resource for new products—can create new industries, generate revenue, and reduce reliance on imported raw materials.

3. Potential Challenges within the Big Idea

From our essential questions, we identified several specific, addressable challenges. One of these will be selected for our project focus.

  1. Inefficient and Hazardous Manual Waste Sorting: The current process at municipal collection points is slow, inaccurate, and poses significant health risks to workers. This leads to low-quality, contaminated recyclables.
  2. E-Waste (Electronic Waste) Management Crisis: There is a lack of a formalized, safe, and efficient system for collecting, dismantling, and extracting valuable materials from e-waste, leading to toxic materials leaching into the environment.
  3. Optimizing Waste Collection Logistics: Municipal waste collection routes are often static and inefficient, leading to high fuel consumption, overflowing bins in some areas, and unnecessary trips in others.
  4. Agricultural/Organic Waste Valorization: A significant portion of waste is organic. Developing small-to-medium scale systems to convert this waste into biogas or high-quality compost at a local level is a major challenge.
  5. Plastic Recycling and Repurposing: Specifically addressing the challenge of low-value plastics (like sachets and food wrappers) that are not typically recycled and end up polluting the environment.
