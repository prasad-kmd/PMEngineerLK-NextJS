1. Ideas Related to Mechanical and Mechatronics Engineering

A. Sustainable Agriculture and Smart Farming

  • Big Idea: Modernizing small-scale agriculture using automation and low-cost technology.

  • Possible Issues:

    • Farmers still depend heavily on manual irrigation and pesticide application.
    • Limited access to affordable automation systems.

  • Example Challenges:

    • Develop an automated soil-moisture-based irrigation system that uses local sensors and low-power controllers.
    • Design a low-cost smart sprayer or drone-based fertilizer distributor for small farms.

B. Waste Management and Recycling

  • Big Idea: Improving the efficiency of waste sorting and recycling systems.

  • Possible Issues:

    • Improper separation of plastic, organic, and metal waste.
    • Manual handling causes health and efficiency problems.

  • Example Challenges:

    • Create a semi-automated waste sorting system using simple mechanical design and image detection.
    • Develop a plastic shredder and compactor for community-level recycling units.

C. Transportation and Vehicle Efficiency

  • Big Idea: Addressing inefficiencies and safety issues in local transport.

  • Possible Issues:

    • Poor maintenance and fuel inefficiency in three-wheelers and buses.
    • Unsafe driving and poor vehicle diagnostics.

  • Example Challenges:

    • Build a smart fuel monitoring and maintenance alert system for three-wheelers.
    • Design a low-cost retrofit kit for electric conversion of small vehicles or motorcycles.

D. Manufacturing and Automation

  • Big Idea: Increasing automation in small-scale industries.

  • Possible Issues:

    • Many small factories rely entirely on manual operation due to the high cost of automation.

  • Example Challenges:

    • Develop a modular low-cost robotic arm or CNC system for small manufacturers.
    • Implement sensor-based quality control for small-scale packaging or assembly lines.

E. Energy Efficiency and Renewable Systems

  • Big Idea: Promoting renewable and efficient energy systems for rural or urban use.

  • Possible Issues:

    • Power cuts and high electricity costs.
    • Underutilization of solar and biomass energy.

  • Example Challenges:

    • Develop a solar-powered water heating or cooling system optimized for local conditions.
    • Design a small-scale wind or hybrid energy system for rural schools or homes.

2. Ideas Outside Your Direct Field (for a broader experience)

A. Public Health and Safety

  • Big Idea: Using engineering thinking to improve healthcare logistics and monitoring.

  • Possible Issues:

    • Long waiting times in clinics, poor data handling, or medicine shortages.

  • Example Challenges:

    • Design a smart queue management system for hospitals.
    • Build a portable biomedical monitoring device for rural patients (temperature, pulse, etc.).

B. Disaster Management and Climate Adaptation

  • Big Idea: Engineering responses to floods, landslides, and droughts.

  • Possible Issues:

    • Frequent flooding and poor early-warning systems.

  • Example Challenges:

    • Create a low-cost flood alert sensor network for rural areas.
    • Develop a modular shelter or mechanical flood barrier for quick deployment.

C. Accessibility and Inclusive Design

  • Big Idea: Making public spaces and tools accessible to all citizens.

  • Possible Issues:

    • People with disabilities face difficulty in movement or using public transport.

  • Example Challenges:

    • Develop an assistive robotic walker or automated wheelchair mechanism.
    • Design a low-cost lift or ramp system adaptable for small shops or homes.

D. Education and Skill Development

  • Big Idea: Supporting technical education through interactive or automated tools.

  • Possible Issues:

    • Lack of hands-on learning tools in rural schools.

  • Example Challenges:

    • Create a low-cost mechanical trainer kit to teach school students basic robotics or mechanics.

How to Narrow Down

  1. Pick a theme that your team genuinely finds meaningful (e.g., sustainability, transport, agriculture).
  2. Identify a local context—a real need or inefficiency you can observe or measure in your own town or district.
  3. From that, define the issue and later shape it into a challenge statement such as: “How might we design an affordable automated irrigation system for small farms in dry zones of Sri Lanka?”
