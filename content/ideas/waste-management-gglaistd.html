1. Why selected this as a big idea?

This idea was selected because Sri Lanka faces substantial challenges with municipal solid waste due to population growth, urbanization, and changing consumption patterns. Open dumping remains a primary disposal method, leading to significant environmental pollution, health hazards, and loss of valuable resources. Issues like inadequate infrastructure, lack of waste segregation, and insufficient funding create a critical need for innovative engineering solutions. For a team of mechanical and mechatronics engineers, this field offers immense opportunity to design and automate systems that can tackle these problems directly, contributing to a more sustainable, circular economy by turning waste into a resource.

2. List the “Essential Questions” considered within the Big Idea that led to a number of challenges.

To explore this broad topic, we considered several key questions that highlight the underlying problems in the Sri Lankan context:

  • How can we improve the sorting of mixed municipal waste to make recycling more efficient and economically viable?
  • What are the most effective small-scale, decentralized methods for processing organic waste, which makes up a large portion of the country's municipal waste?
  • How can technology address the growing problem of electronic waste (e-waste) and allow for the safe recovery of valuable materials?
  • What engineering solutions can convert the high volume of plastic waste, particularly single-use plastics, into valuable new products?
  • How can automation improve the safety and efficiency of waste collection and handling processes?
  • What systems are needed to effectively manage and recycle construction and demolition waste, which is often disposed of in landfills?

3. List all challenges in the Big Idea

Based on the essential questions, here are several specific engineering challenges:

  1. Automated Sorting of Mixed Recyclables: Design a low-cost, automated system using sensors and actuators to separate plastics (like PET and HDPE), paper, metal, and glass from a commingled waste stream.
  2. Decentralized Organic Waste Processor: Develop a compact, automated anaerobic digester or composter for use at a community or municipal level to convert food and garden waste into biogas and fertilizer.
  3. E-Waste Component Recovery System: Create a semi-automated workstation for the safe disassembly of electronic waste (e.g., mobile phones, computers) to harvest and sort valuable components like printed circuit boards and metals.
  4. Plastic Waste-to-Filament Production Unit: Design and build an integrated machine that cleans, shreds, melts, and extrudes common plastic waste (like PET bottles) into high-quality 3D printer filament.
  5. Smart Waste Collection and Logistics System: Develop an IoT-based system with sensors in public bins to monitor fill levels and software to optimize collection routes for municipal trucks, saving fuel and time.
  6. Mobile Construction Debris Recycler: Design a portable machine that can crush and sort concrete, brick, and other construction debris on-site into aggregates of different sizes for reuse in new construction projects.

Let's proceed assuming your team selects Challenge #4: Plastic Waste-to-Filament Production Unit.

4. Why selected that challenge?

This challenge was selected for three key reasons. First, plastic pollution is a severe and highly visible environmental issue in Sri Lanka, with a large volume of plastic waste being mismanaged. This project directly addresses the problem by upcycling a prevalent waste material into a valuable product. Second, the project is an excellent fit for the combined skills of mechanical and mechatronics engineering students, involving material processing, thermal systems, extrusion mechanics, sensor integration, and real-time control systems. Third, the outcome has strong practical value. Locally produced, low-cost 3D printer filament can support innovation in local schools, universities, and small businesses, creating a tangible link between waste management and economic empowerment.

5. List guiding questions

To effectively tackle this challenge, your team should focus on answering these guiding questions:

  1. Which common plastic waste types in Sri Lanka (e.g., PET, HDPE, PP) are most suitable for conversion into 3D printer filament based on their material properties and availability?
  2. What is the necessary pre-processing procedure (cleaning, drying, shredding) to prepare the recovered plastic for a consistent extrusion process?
  3. How can we design a reliable and safe extrusion system that can melt the plastic and force it through a nozzle at a consistent rate?
  4. What combination of sensors and control algorithms is needed to precisely monitor and maintain the filament's diameter (e.g., to 1.75 mm ±0.05 mm) in real-time?
  5. How can the extruded filament be cooled and spooled uniformly to prevent deformation and ensure it is usable in a standard 3D printer?
  6. What tests are required to validate the quality and consistency of the final filament product?

6. Activities, resources, and finding for guiding questions

1. Material Suitability & Sourcing

  • Activities: Collect samples of common plastic waste (PET bottles, bottle caps, etc.). Research their thermal and mechanical properties (melting point, viscosity). Consult with local recycling centers.
  • Resources: University material science lab, online academic journals, local waste collectors.
  • Finding: A decision matrix that identifies PET as the primary target material due to its widespread availability and suitable properties, and a report on sourcing strategies.

2. Pre-processing Procedure

  • Activities: Develop and document a repeatable process for washing, removing labels, drying, and shredding the plastic into uniform flakes.
  • Resources: Sinks, cleaning agents, a low-temperature oven for drying, and a custom-built or purchased plastic shredder.
  • Finding: A standardized operating procedure for converting plastic bottles into clean, dry flakes ready for the extruder.

3. Extrusion System Design

  • Activities: Design the extruder using CAD software. This includes the hopper, barrel, auger screw, heater bands, and nozzle. Fabricate or procure the necessary components.
  • Resources: CAD software (SolidWorks, Fusion 360), university machine shop (lathe, welder), and suppliers for motors, screws, and heating elements.
  • Finding: A complete set of mechanical drawings and a fabricated, assembled extruder mechanism.

4. Diameter Control System

  • Activities: Select and interface an optical or laser diameter sensor with a microcontroller (e.g., Arduino). Write a PID (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) control loop that adjusts the speed of the spooling motor based on the sensor's feedback to maintain constant filament thickness.
  • Resources: Microcontroller, motor driver, optical sensor, programming environment, power supply.
  • Finding: A functional mechatronic system capable of real-time filament diameter measurement and control, with data logging to prove its accuracy.

5. Cooling and Spooling Mechanism

  • Activities: Design and build a system using fans or a water bath to cool the filament as it exits the nozzle. Design a motorized spooler that pulls the filament at a variable speed (controlled by the sensor system).
  • Resources: DC motors, fans, 3D printed parts for the spool holder and guide paths.
  • Finding: An integrated cooling and spooling system that works in tandem with the extruder and control system to produce a neatly wound spool of filament.

6. Quality Validation

  • Activities: Print standard test objects (e.g., calibration cubes, temperature towers) with the produced filament. Measure the dimensional accuracy of the prints. Conduct tensile strength tests on the filament.
  • Resources: A standard 3D printer, calipers, and a materials testing machine if available.
  • Finding: A final report validating the quality of the recycled filament, comparing its performance to commercial-grade filament, and outlining potential areas for improvement.
